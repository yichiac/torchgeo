From PASTIS to North Rhine-Westphalia:
A Practical Walkthrough of Cross-Domain Crop-Type Mapping with a TorchGeo ConvLSTM
==================================================================================


ABSTRACT
--------

We document the design, implementation, and operational lessons of a small
software pipeline that takes a TorchGeo ConvLSTM segmenter trained on the
French PASTIS-R dataset and applies it, without fine-tuning, to crop-type
mapping over a region of Münsterland in North Rhine-Westphalia, Germany.
Sentinel-2 L2A imagery is streamed directly from the Microsoft Planetary
Computer STAC catalogue, predictions are evaluated against EuroCrops
DE_NRW_2021 labels through a harmonised eight-class common crop set, and
results are visualised alongside the first two acquisitions of the input
time-series. The pipeline is intentionally compact (under four hundred
lines of Python) and is intended as both a reproducible demonstration and
a record of the engineering trade-offs that arise when a model trained on
one country is asked to make sense of another.


1. INTRODUCTION
---------------

Cross-domain transfer is one of the most ordinary questions a practitioner
asks of a remote-sensing model: having spent compute and curation effort on
a labelled corpus in country A, does the resulting model carry any utility
in country B? The PASTIS-R dataset, distributed as part of TorchGeo, makes
the question concrete. PASTIS-R aggregates 2433 Sentinel-2 time-series
patches over the southern half of metropolitan France, annotated with a
twenty-class semantic label set that includes the most common European
crops. A ConvLSTM trained on these sequences learns to combine
phenological cues from approximately forty acquisitions per patch into a
parcel-level crop-type map.

In this article we walk through the implementation of
"pastis_to_germany_inference.py", a script that reuses the most recent
checkpoint produced by the companion training script
"spatiotemporal_segmentation_full.py" and asks it to map crops over a
small piece of NRW. The motivation is twofold. First, France-to-Germany
transfer is interesting in its own right because the two countries share
many crops but differ in rotation timing, parcel size, and the relative
prevalence of grassland versus arable cover. Second, the exercise stresses
the entire TorchGeo composition: spatial datasets, vector labels, geospatial
sampling, and a spatiotemporal trainer must all cooperate without code
changes upstream. What follows is, in effect, a field test of those
abstractions.


2. BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
------------------------------

The PASTIS panoptic agricultural time-series benchmark was introduced in
Garnot and Landrieu (2021) and extended to PASTIS-R with paired Sentinel-1
acquisitions. Its labels, painted from the French parcel registry, span
twenty classes including soft and durum wheats, two barleys, corn, rapeseed,
sunflower, beet, grapevine, leguminous fodder, and a residual "void" class
for parcels that fall outside the patch footprint.

EuroCrops is a complementary effort that harmonises self-reported parcel
data from European member states into a single hierarchical taxonomy, the
Harmonised Crop Hierarchical Aggregation Taxonomy (HCAT). The TorchGeo
distribution exposes EuroCrops as a VectorDataset and ships, among others,
two German subsets: Lower Saxony (DE_LS_2021) and North Rhine-Westphalia
(DE_NRW_2021). Neither directly borders France, and neither covers the
specific régions agricoles seen in PASTIS-R. This constraint becomes
important when choosing an evaluation area.

Microsoft's Planetary Computer hosts the full Sentinel-2 L2A archive as
cloud-optimised GeoTIFFs, accessible through a public STAC catalogue
without authentication. Importantly, PC harmonises the +1000 BOA
reflectance offset that ESA introduced in baseline 04.00, so all dates can
be processed on the original 0-10000 surface-reflectance scale without
date-conditional offsets.


3. METHODOLOGY
--------------

3.1 Source model

The trained network is a ConvLSTM segmentation head wrapped by
SpatioTemporalSegmentationTask, with ten input channels (PASTIS S2 bands
B02 through B12, omitting the atmospheric/cirrus and 60 m water-vapour
bands), twenty output classes, a hidden dimension of 128, two recurrent
layers, and 3x3 convolutional kernels in both recurrent and head paths.
Training used a square-root-inverse-frequency class weighting computed over
the entire PASTIS-R semantic mask histogram; this milder weighting was a
deliberate departure from pure inverse-frequency, which produced mode
collapse during pilot runs.

3.2 Area and period of interest

We chose Münsterland, North Rhine-Westphalia, as the operating area. A
naïve reading of the brief would point to Saarland or Baden-Württemberg,
which physically border France and are climatically closer to PASTIS-R.
However, those Bundesländer are not represented in the EuroCrops version
that ships with TorchGeo, and constructing quantitative evaluation without
labels was not practical for this study. NRW, in particular its Münsterland
sub-region, is the closest labelled German subset to France and offers
broad continuous farmland with a rich crop mix dominated by maize, winter
wheat, winter barley, rapeseed, and grassland.

The Sentinel-2 time window was set to 1 September 2020 through 30 November
2021, approximately fifteen months. This range matches the typical
agricultural-year span of PASTIS-R sequences (roughly September of year
N-1 through November of year N) and captures the full phenological cycle
relevant to a 2021 crop-year evaluation: winter-crop emergence and
over-wintering, spring green-up, summer growth, and harvest. Pairing 2021
labels with 2021-vintage imagery, rather than 2018 imagery to match
PASTIS-R training, is the necessary trade-off: crop rotations are
year-specific, so labels and imagery must come from the same year.

3.3 Data acquisition

The script issues a single pystac-client query against the
"sentinel-2-l2a" collection with a bounding box derived from the AOI, the
requested time window, and a scene-level cloud-cover filter of below
twenty percent. Returned items are deduplicated by acquisition date,
keeping the lowest-cloud item per date; if more than thirty-eight items
remain after deduplication, they are evenly sub-sampled to thirty-eight to
match the padding length used during training. For each retained item,
the ten PASTIS bands are read through GDAL's vsicurl driver and reprojected
on the fly into a common UTM 32N target grid at ten-metre resolution using
bilinear resampling. The native ten- and twenty-metre bands are thereby
co-registered without any intermediate file being written, while the
output stack remains in memory as a single (T, C, H, W) float32 tensor.

3.4 Normalisation

PASTIS-R surface reflectance is stored as digital numbers where DN equals
reflectance times ten thousand, and the corresponding PASTIS datamodule
applies a Kornia normalisation with mean zero and standard deviation
10000. Because Planetary Computer harmonises L2A values onto the same
scale, our inference normalisation reduces to a single division by ten
thousand on the input chips. No offset correction is required for our
time window, since it precedes ESA processing baseline 04.00. A point
worth emphasising is that PASTIS-R training does not pre-filter or
SCL-mask cloud pixels; cloudiness is part of the input distribution, and
the ConvLSTM has implicitly learned to discount contaminated timesteps.
For this reason we do not apply pixel-level SCL masking at inference; doing
so would introduce a synthetic pattern the model never observed in
training.

3.5 Label harmonisation

The PASTIS twenty-class space and the EuroCrops HCAT taxonomy do not align
class-for-class. We define an eight-class common crop set: background
(non-crop), wheat, barley, corn, rapeseed, grassland, other crop, and a
"void" class used as the loss/ignore index. Both label spaces are mapped
into this common set. The PASTIS-to-common mapping is a fixed lookup
table; for example, winter triticale is pragmatically folded into wheat
because the original model had limited recall on triticale and the crop is
biologically a wheat-rye hybrid. The EuroCrops mapping is implemented as
a forty-line subclass, EuroCropsCommon, that overrides the framework's
_load_class_map and get_label hooks. Rather than walking the HCAT
hierarchical-code lookup that the stock EuroCrops uses, the subclass
matches against the human-readable EC_hcat_n attribute via a short rule
list. This keeps the mapping logic in one place and lets the inference
script perform the polygon rasterisation through a single bbox query into
the dataset, returning an (H, W) mask in the desired class space.

3.6 Inference protocol

The model was trained on 128x128 chips. For a 10240 m square AOI, this
yields a 1024x1024 pixel grid that we tile into sixty-four
non-overlapping 128x128 chips. Each chip is fed forward independently
with the full T-step input and an explicit per-batch lengths argument that
declares the valid sequence length; ConvLSTM internally uses this length
to select the final hidden state. Predictions are stitched without
overlap blending. The output prediction in PASTIS class indices is then
remapped to the common set through a NumPy lookup.

3.7 Evaluation

We compute pixel-wise overall accuracy and per-class intersection-over-
union, precision, and recall over the subset of pixels whose EuroCrops
class is not the "void" ignore index. A four-panel summary figure is
produced for each AOI: the first two acquisition dates as RGB composites
(to give a visual sanity check that the input series is plausible),
followed by the predicted crop map and the EuroCrops ground truth in the
common set, with a shared colour key. We deliberately chose to display
the first two timesteps rather than a season-median composite, because a
median collapses the very phenology the model exploits and obscures
whether the series begins in the right part of the year.


4. IMPLEMENTATION NOTES
-----------------------

The pipeline is implemented as a single Python script,
"pastis_to_germany_inference.py", with three layers of TorchGeo usage
visible: SpatioTemporalSegmentationTask.load_from_checkpoint for model
restoration, the EuroCrops VectorDataset for label rasterisation, and
torchgeo.datasets.utils for incidental utilities. The Sentinel-2 path
remains based on raw pystac-client and rasterio because TorchGeo does not
ship a STAC client; integrating one would have required a larger surface
change. As a consequence, the script is split roughly into a streaming
acquisition section, a torchgeo-driven label section, an inference loop,
and a metrics/visualisation tail.

Two operational issues emerged during early runs and are worth recording.
The first was wall-clock: the initial implementation streamed all 380 COG
reads (thirty-eight timesteps times ten bands) sequentially, which is
latency-dominated and slow over high-RTT links. We addressed this by
threading the inner band loop with a ten-worker ThreadPoolExecutor, which
yields a five-to-tenfold improvement because GDAL releases the GIL during
HTTP I/O. The second issue was opacity of failure: even after threading,
a multi-AOI run was killed without a recoverable signal or log message.
Two mitigations were put in place. A small log_mem helper now reports
resident-set size and CUDA allocation at four checkpoints in each AOI
loop; the last printed checkpoint before a kill should locate the
offending stage. The 1.5 GB float32 time-series tensor is also explicitly
released after inference, with only the first two frames retained for the
visualisation. After this hardening, the AOI list was temporarily reduced
to a single area until a clean run is observed.

A per-AOI disk cache, written as uint16 to roughly halve the storage
footprint, allows reruns to skip the entire streaming step. The cache key
incorporates centre, size, time window, and cloud parameters, so any
change of those values transparently triggers a re-fetch.


5. DISCUSSION
-------------

The exercise illustrates several recurring themes in applied earth-
observation work. The first is that label-side constraints often
dominate site selection. The naive choice of Karlsruhe at the French
border, which would have been climatologically nearest to PASTIS-R, was
infeasible because TorchGeo does not ship EuroCrops labels for that area.
Falling back on Münsterland trades climatological proximity for the
ability to compute meaningful metrics. The second theme is that
"normalisation" is rarely a single line of code: pre-baseline-04
harmonisation, reflectance scaling, resampling for mixed-resolution
bands, and the question of cloud filtering each have to be reconciled
against the assumptions baked into the trained model. The third is the
slightly counter-intuitive observation that an inference pipeline can be
"cleaner than training" and that this is not necessarily an improvement;
because PASTIS-R training did not pre-filter cloudy acquisitions, removing
them at inference would introduce a new distribution shift rather than
fixing one.

The script also serves as a small case study in how far TorchGeo
abstractions can carry a non-canonical workflow. EuroCrops integrated
cleanly through a forty-line subclass; the trainer's checkpoint-loading
machinery handled the spatiotemporal model without ceremony; the most
friction came at the boundary between STAC and the file-oriented
RasterDataset hierarchy.


6. OPEN QUESTIONS AND FUTURE WORK
---------------------------------

Several extensions are natural. First, identifying the cause of the
multi-AOI kill would allow us to scale evaluation to several places in
Münsterland and aggregate per-class metrics across them, increasing
statistical strength. Second, the current scene-level cloud filter at
twenty percent is conservative relative to training; relaxing the filter,
or removing it entirely, would more faithfully match the PASTIS-R input
distribution and should be tested. Third, the eight-class common set is
a coarse harmonisation; a finer mapping that retained for example
distinct winter and spring barley, or grouped soybean separately from
"other crop", would expose more of the model's capability. Fourth, a
short fine-tuning pass on a small German subset would allow the
zero-shot baseline to be contrasted with a domain-adapted variant and
would directly address the most obvious failure mode of cross-domain
transfer, namely that German crop rotations and parcel sizes differ
systematically from those in southern France. Finally, replacing the
nested-loop tile inference with a GridGeoSampler-driven DataLoader over
a torchgeo Sentinel2 dataset, as in the companion
"pastis_to_germany_inference_torchgeo.py" variant, would consolidate
the spatial bookkeeping into framework code at the cost of an explicit
on-disk imagery cache.


7. CONCLUSION
-------------

We presented a compact pipeline that exercises an existing PASTIS-trained
ConvLSTM on previously unseen ground in North Rhine-Westphalia. The
implementation reads Sentinel-2 reflectance directly from a public STAC
catalogue, uses a torchgeo EuroCrops subclass to obtain ground-truth
crop labels under a harmonised eight-class taxonomy, and reports both
quantitative metrics and a four-panel visualisation per area of interest.
The work surfaces a number of practical concerns - choosing an AOI that
has labels, harmonising mixed taxonomies, matching normalisation to
training assumptions, handling sequential network I/O - that are at least
as central to honest cross-domain evaluation as the model itself. Future
work will focus on widening the geographic coverage once the operational
issues are resolved, on a more careful treatment of cloud pixels, and on
contrasting zero-shot transfer with light fine-tuning.


SOFTWARE AND DATA AVAILABILITY
------------------------------

The inference script and a parallel torchgeo-idiomatic variant are
available alongside this article as "pastis_to_germany_inference.py" and
"pastis_to_germany_inference_torchgeo.py". A running ledger of design
decisions, deviations, and open questions is maintained in
"implementation-notes.html". The training script is
"spatiotemporal_segmentation_full.py". Sentinel-2 imagery is sourced
from the Microsoft Planetary Computer ("sentinel-2-l2a"); EuroCrops is
distributed by the EuroCrops project on Zenodo and is downloaded
automatically by the torchgeo wrapper on first use.
